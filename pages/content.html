For my topic, I choose to explore a potential user problem of finding an art studio. I noticed a large gap in the digital space for a single place to locate art studios as there were no effective digital solutions to be found online. What I did find, however, were many Facebook groups, some forums and dedicated profiles on Instagram. From my own experience, it has largely been through word of mouth that I have managed to find a suitable studio for my needs. This user problem presented an ideal candidate to explore content design in detail as it covers a wide range of user needs and concerns.

How Do Users Speak?

To find out who my potential users are, I have to understand how my users communicate around the topic. This helps to create a mental model of our users, or rather, what words our users might initially think of around the user problem. One of the main purposes of Content Design is to help our users fulfil a need, which could range from ordering a new TV to booking a Hotel for your upcoming holiday. To fulfil that need, a user normally begins with research on the chosen topic. In this instance, however, since I am aiming to create a digital solution, Google will form the first initial step in the user journey. I have narrowed down a list of keywords which my users may use to search for an art studio online. By using Google Trends, I can provide a way to track the usage of keywords over time. This gives me the most commonly used terms regarding my topic.

A User Journey:

The purpose of a user journey is essentially to describe the story or narrative that the user follows throughout their core problem. This helps me to create a clear problem definition and to iterate diverse sets of processes the user may use to fulfil their need. In my case study, I'm attempting to understand how users might find an art studio, which seems simple enough, however, many users will have diverse concerns and approaches to solving their core problem. Lastly, It’s important to consider both the user’s offline and online steps throughout a user journey, this helps me to consider how I could accommodate the user’s transition from offline to online more seamlessly.

Problem Definition: “I want to find an Artist Studio.”

Research Stage - Beginning of the user’s problem.

  • The user is researching art studios on Google in their selected location.
  • The user may be signed up to: Facebook groups, Instagram profiles and any dedicated websites.
  • Has a budget or a price in mind for the studio
  • Is looking for a studio around a specific time or start date.
  • Wants to know if the space is suitable for their artistic practice.
  • Want to know if the space has ventilation, good access to sunlight or workshop tools.
  • Wants the studio to be close to where they live, so they can commute easily, especially if they don't have a car and need to transport materials.
  • Wants to work with like-minded artists, if it is a shared space.

Action Stage - The middle stage of the user’s problem.

  • The user has contacted an entity, person or organisation for a studio.
  • Has found one which meets her price expectations.
  • The studio is available, it might be shared or a singular studio.
  • It is close to her home.
  • The user visits the art studio, or a range of others to view the space and to see if it meets her needs or any other additional needs.
  • The user confirms it is suitable.
  • User meets with the studio provider, either face-to-face, phone call or video call.
  • A contract may be signed or a verbal agreement may be reached.
  • The user pays a deposit and organises her finances for upcoming payments with the studio provider.
  • The user meets the studio provider and picks up the keys for the studio.

Getting State - The end of the user’s problem.

  • The user moves her equipment to the studio.
  • The user is fully moved in and can begin creative work.

Results:

As I understand this topic, users might conduct a lot of research to find the right studio. Also, there is a wide range of needs and specifications for finding a studio space, such as price, location, facilities and diverse additional needs which are not often listed on studio spaces online. The general process is a long and complicated one and would require a lot of work from a user to find a suitable space. Most of the work is in the beginning stage for this reason, this is where emphasis would need to be placed to make the user experience of the content accessible and easy to understand. Through this process, I have discovered how many places a user would have to look for studios, and that it would be beneficial to the user to locate it all in one place.

Discovering User Needs:

By reading through my user journey, I can roughly sketch out possible user needs that relate to my core user problem. I have used the simple “As an…, I need to…, So that I…” to create a rough outline for users needs. This helps me to consider the types of concerns artists may have in finding a studio suitable to their art practice. Then I can start to create a ‘bank of users needs’ to draw from and will help shape my final digital solution.

As an… I need to… So that I… When is it complete…
As an artist who makes large-scale oil paintings. I need to find a large enough space that is suitable and affordable. So that I create more work. It’s complete when I have a large space so that my paintings can fit inside.
As an artist who works with other artists. I need to find a suitable, affordable and shared open studio. So that I create more artistic projects with other people. It’s complete when I am sharing a studio with many other artists.
As an artist who has an upcoming exhibition. I need to find a suitable space to create my work in a short timeframe. So that I create a suitable amount of work for my Dealer Gallery. It’s complete when I have moved into a studio for only three weeks.
As a multidisciplinary artist. I need to find an affordable studio with access to a wide range of production tools. So I create art in many artistic mediums. It’s complete when I have moved into a studio with many production tools and I can use them to make my work.
As an artist who mostly writes text for my practice. I need to find an affordable studio that is quiet and just me. So that I write undisturbed by others and with a clear head for writing. It's complete when I move into a studio that is quiet and I can work peacefully.

From this quick list, I was able to see that there are many diverse needs as reflective of both the diversity of art and artists. Before this, I was primarily concerned with affordability and availability, however, there are many other possible needs the user could be concerned with. Making art requires many possible considerations, for example, an Oil painter would need good access to light and ventilation, and working in a dark space with no ventilation would be damaging to both the body and the creative output. User needs have so far been the most revealing in uncovering the most around my chosen topic and led me to consider other possible user personas. This is not an exhaustive list by no means, and I could add hundreds more to the user needs list, however, it serves to illustrate the importance of constructing possible scenarios for each user's core problem and the range of diversity it may uncover.

Acceptance Criteria:

The purpose of acceptance criteria is to discover what needs to be met to fulfil their core problem definition. It is a record of what must be done for the user’s problem to be resolved and acts essentially as a way to bridge the gap between the user journey and the user's needs.

User Need Example:

As an… artist who makes large-scale oil paintings.

I need to.. find a large enough space that is suitable and affordable.

So that I… continue to create more work.

Acceptance Criteria Example:

  • I need to see an available studio.
  • I want to know the prices of each studio.
  • I want to know if it is shared or for one person.
  • I want to know if the space is big enough for my paintings.
  • I need to be able to arrange and view or make contact.
  • I want to know its location.
  • I want to know if this space is suitable, and has good access to light and ventilation.
  • I want to know when I can move in, if help is available for moving or if it could be easy to access.
  • I want to know the people there or meet them in person if it is a shared studio.
  • I want access to wifi, toilet and access to a small kitchen.
  • I need to see the studio first to check if it is suitable.

Results:

Through documenting acceptance criteria for just one of my user needs, I have discovered that there are many variables for each user and can increasingly become more and more detailed. I have noticed another gap in understanding the available web solutions for this topic and that more options, such as if a studio has good sunlight or ventilation, would be beneficial for a user to know. I have realised that studios are spaces which ideally reflect the user's needs physically, not just simply availability and price. Finally, artists have very diverse sets of user needs, and more consideration is needed to help support them on their user journey. There are, however, many other aspects of studios I hadn't considered, such as having wifi or if it's a shared space.

How We Read:

After looking through my user needs, user journeys and acceptance criteria I will now be able to create an example listing for my digital solution. This listing will be hypothetical and will seek to sketch out what content needs to be addressed to satisfy a wide range of user needs. What are the types of things a studio needs for an artist to be interested? categories such as length, location, facilities and price will help our users to determine if it is suitable. Following well-known criteria from content design, this listing can serve as a template to use in my digital solution. The use of clear headings and subheadings will help my users quickly scan the content since our attention span online only lasts for an average of eight seconds. Finally, content should be easily readable for users, Beth Dunn in her book Cultivating Content Design sums this up perfectly, she states:

“Font size, color contrast, plain language, adaptability to screen readers, and much more all go into designing accessible content. Words need to be readable, in the sense that the user needs to be able to see and read the letters on the screen; and the text needs to be written at a reading level that optimizes comprehension. Text written at a sixth- or seventh-grade reading level is generally the most easily understood by all users, regardless of education level, industry, or background.”

Results:

The title immediately adds the verb of ‘join’ to make clear to the reader an action to resolve their core user problem. I also added the words ‘studio’ and its location ‘London’ to immediately address possible concerns for the user - knowledge of its location and the simple fact it is a studio. From this, I decided to break up the content into clear distinct categories, such as Price and Location, to make it visibly easier to read and could be scanned over quickly. I have also attempted to reduce the content most efficiently, making it easy to understand. Sentences are reduced in length, rather than lengthy and complex ones, and words are substituted for high-frequency words (used most often in speech), this helps our reader to follow the text at an easier pace. Finally, the general tone of the listing attempts to match the voice of my intended audience, as it should be professional, clear and direct in delivery. The tone should not be vague or casual, as this would imply a degree of untrustworthiness for the user since finding a studio is a financial risk and requires investment in time.

Digital Solution:

Based on my user research, I knew quite quickly that the solution to the core problem definition would require a digital search website. From understanding user needs, it should contain a range of search options for users. This will enable users to search for studios based on their unique preferences. It should match in design preexisting websites on finding places to rent while focusing on accessibility and common search functions. I will use cards to display listings, which is a common technique in the tech industry and with various categories based on my listing template so that studio providers can enter their studio details. User interface design should also resemble common search functions so that new users are already familiar with navigating the product rather than attempting to create a new user interface design. I will attempt to focus on clear visual accessibility so that it is clear for partially sighted users. Colours will be kept at a minimum, with an emphasis in certain areas to highlight the hierarchy of importance. Finally, I will use visual research, wireframes and prototypes to arrive at a final digital solution design.

Researching Accessibility:

1. GOV.UK 2. PATREON.COM 3. BBC.CO.UK 4. Excel Accessibility 5. WORDPRESS.ORG

The websites I have chosen are known for their remarkable effort in making their website as accessible as possible. I have noticed there is a wide range of shared characteristics between their designs and implementation. By researching these websites, I can understand what it is visually that makes websites so accessible to the widest range of users.

Accessible Website Characteristics:

  • Content is presented in a range of diverse formats, such as text, video and audio to cater to a wide range of user needs.
  • Website is arranged or ‘Operable’ according to Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG). That means that the website is straightforward in display, menus are laid appropriately, the website can be navigated with screen readers and the content is arranged in an easy-to-understand design pattern.
  • The content of the website is clear and easy to read. Headings, subheadings and text are made bold where appropriate and font size is determined by hierarchy of importance. The text is straightforward and uses high-frequency words. For example, processes such as forms, checkout, button functions, and further information resources are made understandable.
  • The visual layout of the website with the use of minimal colours and design is laid out in such a way that content is easily scanned over for users. Content is easily distinguishable for users, especially for those with visual impairments.
  • Images are uniform in size unless made larger to increase importance.
  • Accommodates a wide range of screen sizes, and browsers, in addition to functionality for screen readers.
  • High readability for users, enhancing their experience of the website.
  • Alt text is used for images
  • Aria labels are used frequently and appropriately.
  • The layout of the website is uniform and doesn't change drastically throughout.
  • Good use of Navigation aids to help users find content more quickly.
  • Use of keyboard accessibility for users who may use a mouse.
  • Accessibility link on the page to highlight features for users.
  • Gives more control over the use of content.
  • Places users first, at the centre of the design.
  • Effective use of line spacing and visually clear button design.
  • Demonstrates a commitment to equal access.
  • Follows Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG), such as Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, and Robust.

Search Tools in Websites, Characteristics:

  • Contains a clear search bar and a search button.
  • Minimal in design and colour.
  • Offers filtering options.
  • Shows the amount of listings in text.
  • Clear title headings, and navigation at the top of the page.
  • Offers search suggestions.
  • Listings are displayed in a grid and using flexbox.
  • Clear titles on listings, easily scannable.
  • Colour is subtle and used in areas to highlight importance.
  • Subtle use of shadows to bring items to the front of the page.
  • Listings have basic use of heading, description, image and price. Can click further to reveal more information.
  • Filters are buttons that can be clicked to offer a range of options.
  • Some use logos for search, but I prefer using clear words or a combination of both.
  • Most of these websites are visually accessible for the partially sighted.
  • Websites in the Art World tend to be black and white, in addition to being minimal.
  • Use of error message in case of failed search.
  • Use of a hint text in the search bar.
  • Colour could be used on the search button.
  • Predictive query and autocomplete Suggestions.
  • A universal symbol of search is used.
  • Strong use of the F-shaped pattern in layout.
  • The search field is long and visible.
